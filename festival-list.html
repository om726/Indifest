Festivals Happening Across India

Festivals in Kerala

Onam

Onam: Harvest festival, celebrated in August or September.

Vishu

Vishu: New Year festival celebrated in April.

Thiruvathira

Thiruvathira: Celebrated in December-January, honoring Goddess Parvati.

Festivals in Maharashtra

Ganesh Chaturthi

Ganesh Chaturthi: Celebrated in August-September to honor Lord Ganesha.

Gudi Padwa

Gudi Padwa: Marathi New Year, celebrated in March-April.

Diwali

Diwali: The Festival of Lights, celebrated in October-November.

Festivals in Punjab

Lohri

Lohri: Harvest festival, celebrated in January.

Baisakhi

Baisakhi: Celebrated in April, marking the harvest season and Sikh New Year.

Diwali

Diwali: Major festival, celebrated in October-November.

Festivals in Uttar Pradesh

Diwali

Diwali: Celebrated in October-November, especially in Ayodhya.

Holi

Holi: Festival of Colors, celebrated in March.

Kumbh Mela

Kumbh Mela: A major pilgrimage and festival, held every 12 years at Prayagraj.

Festivals in Gujarat

Navratri

Navratri: Nine-night festival of dance and worship in September-October.

Diwali

Diwali: Celebrated in October-November, marking the victory of light over darkness.

Uttarayan

Uttarayan: Kite festival celebrated in January.

Festivals in West Bengal

Durga Puja

Durga Puja: Major festival, celebrated in September-October, especially in Kolkata.

Poila Boishakh

Poila Boishakh: Bengali New Year, celebrated in April.

Kali Puja

Kali Puja: Celebrated during Diwali in October-November.

Festivals in Bihar

Chhath Puja

Chhath Puja: A major festival, celebrated in October-November, dedicated to the Sun God.

Sarada Navaratri

Sarada Navaratri: Celebrated in September-October with religious rituals.

Festivals in Tamil Nadu

Pongal

Pongal: Harvest festival, celebrated in January.

Deepavali

Deepavali: The Festival of Lights, celebrated in October-November.

Thaipusam

Thaipusam: Celebrated in January-February, honoring Lord Murugan.

Festivals in Nagaland

Hornbill Festival

Hornbill Festival: A cultural festival held in December to showcase the traditions, arts, and cultures of Nagaland's various tribes.

Moatsu Festival

Moatsu Festival: Celebrated by the Ao tribe in May, marking the end of the sowing season and the beginning of the harvest season.

Sekrenyi Festival

Sekrenyi Festival: Celebrated by the Angami Naga tribe in February, this festival is dedicated to the purification rituals and social harmony.

Tuluni Festival

Tuluni Festival: Celebrated in July by the Sumi tribe, it marks the beginning of the harvest season and includes feasts, dances, and traditional rituals.

Ngada Festival

Ngada Festival: A festival celebrated by the Sumi Naga tribe in November, honoring the spirits of the ancestors and involving rituals, feasts, and cultural activities.

Festivals in Odisha

Rath Yatra

Rath Yatra: One of the most famous festivals in Odisha, celebrated in Puri. It involves the grand procession of Lord Jagannath's chariot, which is pulled by thousands of devotees. It usually takes place in June or July.

Durga Puja

Durga Puja: Celebrated in September-October, it is one of the biggest festivals in Odisha, especially in Cuttack and Bhubaneswar. The festival is dedicated to the worship of Goddess Durga, and large pandals (temporary structures) are erected for the rituals and processions.

Makar Sankranti

Makar Sankranti: Celebrated in January, Makar Sankranti marks the transition of the sun into the zodiac sign of Capricorn. People celebrate with kite flying, feasting, and various cultural events.

Nuakhai

Nuakhai: Celebrated in August or September, this is a harvest festival in western Odisha. It marks the first consumption of newly harvested rice, and people thank the earth and nature for the harvest.

Mahashivaratri

Mahashivaratri: Celebrated in February or March, it is a night-long festival dedicated to Lord Shiva. Devotees fast, meditate, and visit temples to worship Shiva during this auspicious night.

Kartik Purnima

Kartik Purnima: Celebrated in November, it is dedicated to Lord Vishnu, and large-scale rituals are performed at temples, especially in Puri. The festival is also associated with the holy dip in the sea and other rivers.

Festivals in Jharkhand

Jitiya

Jitiya: A major festival celebrated by women in Jharkhand, especially by married women, for the well-being of their children. It falls in the month of September or October and involves fasting and rituals.

Tusu Parab

Tusu Parab: Celebrated in January, this festival marks the arrival of the harvest season. It is widely celebrated in rural Jharkhand with traditional dances and folk music.

Chhath Puja

Chhath Puja: One of the most important festivals for the people of Jharkhand, dedicated to the Sun God. Celebrated in October-November, it involves fasting, bathing in rivers, and offering prayers at sunrise and sunset.

Holi

Holi: Like most of India, Holi is celebrated with much fervor in Jharkhand, in March. The festival of colors symbolizes the victory of good over evil and the arrival of spring.

Karam

Karam: Karam is an indigenous tribal festival celebrated in August or September, primarily by the tribal communities in Jharkhand. It is a festival of nature worship, particularly the Karam tree, where people pray for a good harvest and health.

Diwali

Diwali: The Festival of Lights is widely celebrated in Jharkhand, particularly in urban areas like Ranchi and Jamshedpur, in October-November. People decorate their homes with lights, perform prayers, and enjoy festive feasts.

Festivals in Rajasthan

Pushkar Fair

Pushkar Fair: One of the largest camel and livestock fairs in the world, the Pushkar Fair is held in November in the town of Pushkar. It is famous for its vibrant cultural events, including folk music, dance, and camel races.

Diwali

Diwali: Diwali is celebrated with grandeur across Rajasthan, particularly in Jaipur, Udaipur, and Jodhpur. The festival of lights is marked by decorating homes with diyas (oil lamps), bursting crackers, and performing religious rituals.

Gangaur

Gangaur: Celebrated in March or April, Gangaur is a festival dedicated to Goddess Parvati. Women worship the goddess and celebrate her marital bliss with processions, folk dances, and traditional rituals.

Teej

Teej: A monsoon festival, celebrated in August or September, that honors Goddess Parvati and her marriage to Lord Shiva. Women fast and celebrate with songs, dances, and wearing traditional attire.

Makar Sankranti

Makar Sankranti: Celebrated in January, Makar Sankranti is marked by kite flying, bonfires, and feasts. It is a harvest festival and holds great significance in Rajasthan.

Desert Festival

Desert Festival: Held annually in February in Jaisalmer, the Desert Festival is a colorful celebration of Rajasthani culture, with folk music, dance, camel races, and competitions.

Festivals in Sikkim

Losar

Losar: The Tibetan New Year, celebrated in February or March, is one of the most important festivals in Sikkim. It is celebrated with traditional dances, feasts, and prayers for prosperity and good health.

Maghe Sankranti

Maghe Sankranti: Celebrated in January, Maghe Sankranti marks the beginning of the harvest season in Sikkim. People celebrate with offerings to the Sun God and prepare traditional foods.

Drukpa Tseshi

Drukpa Tseshi: A Buddhist festival celebrated in September, Drukpa Tseshi marks the establishment of Buddhism in Sikkim. Monasteries host prayers, processions, and traditional rituals.

Chhewar

Chhewar: A coming-of-age ceremony celebrated by the Limbu community in Sikkim, usually around the age of 7 to 10 years. It is a significant occasion involving elaborate rituals and prayers.

Independence Day (15th August)

Independence Day (15th August): Though it is a national holiday, Sikkim celebrates Independence Day with cultural events, performances, and flag-hoisting ceremonies, particularly in Gangtok, the state capital.

Dasain (Dussehra)

Dasain (Dussehra): Celebrated in September-October, Dasain is a major Hindu festival observed in Sikkim, especially by the Nepali-speaking population. It marks the victory of good over evil and is celebrated with prayers, feasts, and family gatherings.

Festivals in Telangana

Bathukamma

Bathukamma: A floral festival celebrated in September or October, Bathukamma is a unique festival dedicated to Goddess Gauri, symbolizing the beauty of nature. Women create floral arrangements and immerse them in local water bodies.

Bonalu

Bonalu: A significant festival in Telangana, celebrated in July or August, Bonalu honors Goddess Mahakali. Devotees offer food, including rice and curd, in decorated pots, while participating in processions and prayers.

Ugadi

Ugadi: The Telugu New Year, celebrated in March or April, Ugadi marks the beginning of the Hindu lunar calendar. People decorate their homes, cook special dishes, and visit temples for prayers.

Diwali

Diwali: Like other parts of India, Diwali is widely celebrated in Telangana. The festival of lights is celebrated with decorations, fireworks, and family gatherings.

Festivals in Tripura

Kharchi Puja

Kharchi Puja: Celebrated in July, this is a major religious festival dedicated to the worship of fourteen deities of the Tripura Tribal areas. The main attraction is the grand procession, with tribal dances and traditional music.

Durga Puja

Durga Puja: Tripura, particularly its capital Agartala, celebrates Durga Puja in September or October with much grandeur. The festival includes elaborate rituals, processions, and cultural performances.

Ker Puja

Ker Puja: A festival of the indigenous Tripuri people, celebrated in the month of January or February, Ker Puja is dedicated to the worship of deities of nature and is performed in tribal areas.

Festivals in Uttarakhand

Kumbh Mela

Kumbh Mela: One of the largest religious gatherings in the world, Kumbh Mela takes place every 12 years at Haridwar in Uttarakhand. Devotees come from across the country to take a holy dip in the Ganges River.

Harela

Harela: Celebrated in July or August, Harela is a harvest festival dedicated to nature, during which people plant saplings, pray for prosperity, and enjoy traditional dances and songs.

Shivaratri

Shivaratri: Celebrated in February or March, Mahashivaratri in Uttarakhand is marked by fasting, prayers, and night vigils at temples, especially in places like Kedarnath and Haridwar.

Festivals in Himachal Pradesh

Holi

Holi: Celebrated in March, Holi in Himachal Pradesh is marked by local traditions, especially in the Kullu Valley, where people perform folk dances, songs, and play with colors.

Dussera

Dussera: Dussera is celebrated with much zeal in Kullu in October, where the famous Kullu Dussera is held, featuring large processions, cultural programs, and religious rituals.

Maghi Mela

Maghi Mela: Held in January at the famous Sangla Valley, it is a religious and cultural festival celebrating the traditional lifestyle of the region.

Festivals in Haryana

Teej

Teej: Celebrated in August or September, Teej is a major festival in Haryana where women dress in traditional attire, sing folk songs, and observe fasting for the well-being of their husbands.

Holi

Holi: Like other parts of India, Holi is celebrated with great enthusiasm in Haryana in March. The festival of colors is celebrated with singing, dancing, and playing with colors.

Baisakhi

Baisakhi: Celebrated in April, Baisakhi marks the harvest season and is an important festival for farmers. It is also associated with the formation of the Khalsa by Guru Gobind Singh in Sikhism.

Festivals in Chhattisgarh

Hareli

Hareli: A harvest festival celebrated in August, Hareli is dedicated to the agricultural prosperity of the region. Farmers worship their tools and cattle, and traditional dances and songs are performed.

Kharchi Puja

Kharchi Puja: This festival, celebrated in the Bastar region in July, involves worshipping 14 deities and includes processions, music, and tribal dances.

Chhattisgarhi New Year (Bastar Dussehra)

Chhattisgarhi New Year (Bastar Dussehra): The Bastar Dussehra, celebrated in September or October, is a grand festival in the tribal regions, marking the victory of good over evil. It includes rituals, dance performances, and a grand procession.

Festivals in Goa

Carnival

Carnival: Held in February or March, the Carnival in Goa is a vibrant festival with parades, dances, and music. It reflects the state's Portuguese heritage and is one of the most famous celebrations in the region.

Shigmo

Shigmo: A spring festival celebrated in March, Shigmo is a traditional Goan festival with parades, folk dances, and performances that depict the rich cultural heritage of the region.

Diwali

Diwali: Diwali is celebrated with traditional rituals, lights, and fireworks, especially in Panjim and other parts of Goa. It is a major festival in both Hindu and Christian communities.

Festivals in Assam

Bihu

Bihu: The most important festival of Assam, celebrated in three phases (Bohag Bihu in April, Magh Bihu in January, and Kati Bihu in October). Bihu celebrates the Assamese New Year, the harvest, and the onset of the spring season.

Durga Puja

Durga Puja: Celebrated in September or October, Durga Puja is a major festival in Assam, especially in cities like Guwahati, where colorful decorations, pandals, and processions are seen.

Ambubachi Mela

Ambubachi Mela: Celebrated in June at the Kamakhya Temple, this festival marks the annual menstruation period of Goddess Kamakhya. It attracts thousands of devotees.

Festivals in Arunachal Pradesh

Losar

Losar: The Tibetan New Year, celebrated in February or March, is a significant festival in Arunachal Pradesh, especially among the Monpa people, with prayers, dances, and feasts.

Solung

Solung: Celebrated in September, Solung is a major harvest festival of the Adi tribe of Arunachal Pradesh. It is marked by traditional rituals, songs, dances, and offerings to the deities.

Nyokum

Nyokum: Celebrated in February, this is the festival of the Nyishi tribe and marks the beginning of the agricultural season. It is celebrated with traditional dances, feasts, and rituals.

Festivals in Karnataka

Karaga

Karaga: One of the oldest festivals in Bangalore, Karaga is celebrated in March or April. It is a religious procession held in honor of Draupadi, with rituals and folk performances.

Ugadi

Ugadi: The New Year festival celebrated in March or April, Ugadi in Karnataka is marked by rituals, prayers, and the consumption of a special dish called 'Bevu-Bella' (neem and jaggery mix).

Dasara

Dasara: Karnataka celebrates Dasara with great enthusiasm, especially in Mysore, where a grand procession takes place with elephants, chariots, and folk performances.

Festivals in Madhya Pradesh

Khajuraho Dance Festival

Khajuraho Dance Festival: Held in February-March at the famous Khajuraho temples, this festival celebrates classical dance forms and music, drawing artists and tourists from across the country.

Diwali

Diwali: Diwali is widely celebrated across Madhya Pradesh, with special emphasis on the lighting of lamps and the performance of rituals in temples and homes.

Navratri

Navratri: The nine-day festival dedicated to Goddess Durga is celebrated with traditional dances, especially Garba and Dandiya, in cities like Indore and Bhopal.

Festivals in Manipur

Yaoshang

Yaoshang: Celebrated in February or March, Yaoshang is a Manipuri version of Holi, celebrated with traditional dances, plays, and playing with colors.

Cheiraoba

Cheiraoba: The New Year festival, celebrated in April, involves rituals, family gatherings, and the preparation of traditional food.

Lamta

Lamta: A festival of the Meitei tribe, Lamta is celebrated in autumn with traditional prayers, dances, and rituals to ensure the health and well-being of the community.

Festivals in Meghalaya

Behdienkhlam

Behdienkhlam: Celebrated by the Khasi tribe, Behdienkhlam is a harvest festival that involves rituals, dances, and feasts. It is celebrated in July or August.

Shad Suk Mynsiem

Shad Suk Mynsiem: A major festival of the Khasi people, celebrated in April, Shad Suk Mynsiem is a festival of dances, songs, and thanksgiving for the harvest.

Festivals in Mizoram

Chapchar Kut

Celebrated in March, Chapchar Kut is the most important festival in Mizoram, marking the end of the bamboo cutting season. It is celebrated with traditional dance, music, and feasts.

Thalfavang Kut

Held in November, this festival celebrates the harvest season, with dances, feasts, and offerings to the gods.

Festival Timeline

The following festivals are celebrated at different times throughout the year:

  • January - March: Pongal (Tamil Nadu), Lohri (Punjab), Baisakhi (Punjab), Maha Shivaratri (Across India)
  • April - June: Vishu (Kerala) (nagaland), Baisakhi (Punjab), Gudi Padwa (Maharashtra)
  • July - September: Onam (Kerala), Raksha Bandhan (Across India), Janmashtami (Across India)
  • October - December: Diwali (Across India), Durga Puja (West Bengal), Navratri (Gujarat), Ganesh Chaturthi (Maharashtra)
